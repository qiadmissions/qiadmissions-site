The Method

How the whole thing works.

This is the longer version, for the parent who wants to understand exactly what we do: the idea behind it, the five stages, how we work with one student, and what you actually end up with.

The name is the point

Quintessence means the fifth essence. The old idea was that beyond earth, water, air, and fire there was a fifth, purer element — what's left when you strip away everything that isn't essential. We took the name on purpose, because that's the job.

For most families, the application is an assembly job: fill in the forms, list the activities, write essays to fit the prompts, and hope it adds up. We start somewhere else — before any of that — by helping a student figure out who they actually are, and what's genuinely theirs to say. The application is just where that becomes visible to a stranger. So we're not really in the essay business. We're in the business of a student understanding themselves well enough that the truest version of their story is also the one that gets them in.

Honesty is the strategy

Most admissions advice quietly asks one question: what do they want to hear? It treats the application as a performance. The problem is that admissions officers read thousands of performances a year, and they can tell the difference between a person and an act in the first sentence.

We work the other way, and this is the center of everything: the most strategic story and the most honest story are the same story. What's actually distinctive about a kid — how they really think, the thing they can't stop caring about, the experience that quietly shaped the rest — is usually also the most compelling thing they could put in front of a committee. There's no trade-off between being honest and being effective. There's just the work of finding the true thing and telling it well.

And it's the practical choice, not the sentimental one. A made-up angle has a ceiling — you can polish it, but you can't deepen it, because there's nothing underneath. A true story has no ceiling. The closer you look, the more it gives you.

A method, not a formula

Some college consulting runs on templates — a formula for the personal statement, a stock structure for the supplements, a system that, fed enough students, produces enough acceptances. We get the appeal. A formula is calming; it promises the whole messy thing can be reduced to steps.

But a kid's future isn't a place to gamble, and a person isn't raw material for a formula. Think of it more like cooking. A student shows up with a pantry — everything they've done, felt, gotten through, and gotten curious about. Most of it looks ordinary at first: the move between countries, the after-school job, the obsession nobody else gets, the failure that never quite resolved. The work is in the composition — knowing which pieces belong together, what to lead with and what to hold back, how to make something an admissions officer doesn't want to put down. The ingredients of one life, put together into something true.

The Five Elements

The method has five stages, run in order — from the most open to the most finished. Each has a name for what it is and a name for what the student is doing in it.

SpaceFraming

Set the table. Before we talk about a single essay, we get clear on what colleges are really looking for and on everything that makes this particular student who they are — facts, stories, offhand remarks, things they'd never think to mention. This is also where the school list gets built, alongside the student rather than handed down. We err on the side of gathering too much; the richer the raw material, the truer the result.

What it produces

A clear picture of the student, the right school list, and the overall plan.

FireFocusing

Find the core. Gathering isn't yet a strategy. This is where it becomes one — pulling the scattered pieces into one clear identity, the through-line that makes a whole life suddenly read as one person with one signature. It's the most important thinking in the process, because everything after it follows from here.

What it produces

An archetype — the student's strategic identity — and a clear plan for every essay.

WaterFlowing

The student writes. With the plan in hand, they put it into words — in their own voice. This stage can feel slow, but it's where the best, most surprising material shows up. Our job is to hold the structure steady and keep them writing toward it. The one thing we never do is pick up the pen.

What it produces

First drafts — every word the student's own.

AirFixing

Strengthen the structure. Now we revise — at the level of argument and shape, not commas. Does each essay do its job? Does it still sound like this student? Does it avoid repeating what another essay already says, so the whole thing reads as one application instead of a stack of separate tries?

What it produces

Revised drafts that carry their weight and hold together.

EarthFinalizing

Bring it to a finish. The last pass — line-level polish, every requirement met, every detail clean, ready to submit. We call this finalizing, not finishing, on purpose. Finishing sounds done; finalizing is the honest word for the real work between a strong draft and something worth sending.

What it produces

The final application, ready to submit.

One shape, at every size

The same five stages that shape a single essay shape the whole application — and, for a family that starts early, the student's path from one grade to the next. It's the same method, applied with the same care, at every scale.

One student at a time

All of this is built around a single applicant. We're not a counselor split across hundreds of students, and we're not a tutor brought in for one subject. We're a full advisory built around one kid — which is the only way this kind of depth is possible.

Two things hold the work together. The first is real rapport — knowing a student well enough that they'll tell you the true thing, which is the only thing worth building on. The second is that they stay the author the whole way.

We want to be plain about this, because it's the line we won't cross: your child writes the essays. We don't write a draft for them to approve, and no tool writes it either. We guide; they write. Not us. Not anyone else. Them. Some people hear that as a limit on what we offer. It's the opposite — it's the whole point. It keeps the work honest, keeps the voice unmistakably theirs, and leaves a kid who can stand behind every sentence because every sentence is actually theirs.

What you end up with

Concretely: a clear picture of your child, a strategy that organizes the whole application, a plan for every essay, drafts in their own voice, and a finished application ready to submit.

And underneath that, a student who, by the end, understands themselves a little better than they did at the start — with an application that is genuinely, honestly theirs.

The craft behind it

An application comes down to how clearly the applicant comes through on the page — and that's a question of story, strategy, and writing. That's the craft we're built around.

Adam Obedian, who founded Quintessence, studied Philosophy and Drama at Columbia — the training of a writer and a reader of people, which is exactly what this work needs.

The whole of it: help a young person figure out who they really are — then put that on the page so honestly that honesty becomes the smartest move they could make.
